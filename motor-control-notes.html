1. Motor types — construction & how each one works

DC brushed motor

Construction: permanent magnets (or field winding) on the stator (fixed), armature winding on the rotor (spinning part), connected to the supply through a commutator + brushes — physical contacts that switch the winding's current direction as the rotor turns.

Back-EMF: as the rotor spins through the magnetic field, it generates its own voltage (E = Ke·ω) that opposes the supply voltage. Net current = (Va − E)/Ra — this is why current drops as speed rises, and why stall current (ω=0, E=0) is the highest current the motor ever sees: I_stall = Va/Ra.

Torque: T = Kt·Ia (in SI units, Kt = Ke numerically).

Why poor thermal performance: the armature winding (where I²R losses happen) is on the ROTOR — heat generated there has to escape through the rotor shaft/bearings, which is a poor heat path compared to a stator winding that touches the motor housing directly.

BLDC motor (brushless DC)

Construction: the OPPOSITE arrangement from brushed DC — permanent magnets on the rotor, windings on the stator. No brushes/commutator at all.

Electronic commutation: since the windings don't physically rotate, something needs to switch which winding is energized as the rotor turns. A controller does this electronically, using rotor-position feedback (Hall sensors or an encoder) to know exactly when to switch each phase.

Why better thermal performance than brushed DC: the windings (where I²R losses happen) are now on the STATOR, which is in direct contact with the motor housing/heatsink — much easier to cool than a spinning rotor.

Trade-off: needs a more complex controller (position sensing + multi-phase switching electronics) compared to a simple H-bridge for brushed DC.

Stepper motor

Construction: many small magnetic poles (rotor + stator), driven by sequential current pulses through multiple windings — each pulse rotates the shaft by one fixed "step" angle.

Open-loop positioning: because each step is a known, fixed angle, the controller can just count how many step pulses it sent to know the shaft position — no feedback sensor required (though one can be added for verification).

Trade-off: high torque ripple (torque varies noticeably between steps — vibration/noise), and if the load ever exceeds the motor's torque at a given step (a "missed step"), the open-loop position tracking becomes wrong with no way to detect it.

Synchronous reluctance motor (SyncRM)

Construction: stator windings like an induction/BLDC motor, but the rotor has NO magnets and NO windings — just a specially shaped iron core that prefers to align with the rotating stator field (minimizing magnetic reluctance).

Why it shows up in exams as a distractor: no permanent magnets means no demagnetization risk and lower cost, but it needs more sophisticated vector control (similar to FOC) to run smoothly — it's a common wrong-answer option in "identify this motor" questions because it doesn't match the "simple controller" trait that points to brushed DC.

3. H-bridge & PWM driving

Topology: 4 switches (2 legs) around the motor — diagonal pairs switch together to drive current one way or the other through the motor, giving forward/reverse and (with all 4 switches) regenerative braking capability.

Recurring question

"What does the series inductor L do?" → Smooths the PWM-chopped output into a steadier current — it's a low-pass filter on current, reducing ripple caused by the switching. NOT for storing regen energy, NOT for fault current limiting (common wrong-answer distractors).

Flyback/freewheeling diodes: each switch has one in anti-parallel, giving the motor's inductive current a path to flow when a switch turns off — without them, switching off an inductive load creates a damaging voltage spike (L·di/dt).

PWM duty cycle: average voltage across the motor ≈ duty cycle × supply voltage. Higher duty cycle → higher average voltage → higher speed (at a given load).

4. Four-quadrant operation & regenerative braking

Quadrant logic: if speed and torque have the SAME sign, the motor is driving the load (motoring). If they have OPPOSITE signs, the motor is being driven BY the load (generating/braking) — the machine acts as a generator, converting kinetic energy back to electrical energy.

Recurring trap

"Regenerative braking means the motor doesn't heat up." → False. Even while regenerating, current still flows through the armature resistance, so I²R copper losses still occur — the motor still heats up. Regen braking is about energy recovery (vs. dumping it in a brake resistor), not about eliminating heat generation.

5. SCR/thyristor switching

How it works: a 4-layer PNPN device. Once gate-triggered while forward-biased, it latches ON — current keeps flowing even after the gate pulse is removed. It only turns OFF when the current through it drops below the holding current (I_H).

Recurring question

"Can an SCR-based switch handle DC loads?" → No, AC only. For DC, current never naturally crosses zero, so there's no moment where the SCR's current drops below I_H — once triggered, it stays on forever (or until you forcibly interrupt the circuit some other way). AC current crosses zero every half-cycle, giving a natural turn-off point.

6. The big combined thermal + motor calculation

This is the highest-value open question type (15-16 points in past exams) — it chains motor torque/current relationships together with thermal limits. Here's the general method, since the EXACT scenario will likely change:

Step 1 — find the relevant current. This could be: stall current (I = Va/Ra, at ω=0), nominal/rated current (I = T_nominal/Kt), or overload current (often given as a % above nominal, or recalculated from an overload torque/power figure). Read carefully which scenario the question describes — this is the step most likely to change between exam versions.
Step 2 — copper losses. P_copper = I² × Ra. If the question also gives "no-load losses" or friction torque × ω, ADD that mechanical/iron loss term too — don't forget it (seen in the November exam's part (c), where final temperature combined I²R AND friction torque × ω).
Step 3 — steady-state temperature rise. ΔT = P_total × Rth. Add ambient temperature to get the actual winding temperature.
Step 4 — check against the limit. Compare against the insulation class max (B=130°C, F=155°C, H=180°C) or a given max winding temperature. If it's exceeded, the motor can't run continuously at that load — that's where the thermal TIME CONSTANT comes in.
Step 5 — if it's a transient/duty-cycle question: use θ(t) = θ_start + (θ_final − θ_start)(1 − e−t/τ) to find either the temperature after a given time, OR (more commonly asked) solve for the MAXIMUM TIME t before the winding would exceed its limit, by rearranging for t: t = −τ · ln(1 − (T_limit − θ_start)/(θ_final − θ_start)).

Worked-style walkthrough (generic numbers, same method as the real exams):
Given: Kt = 0.05 N·m/A, Ra = 1.2 Ω, Rth = 14°C/W, τ_th = 28 s, ambient = 25°C, insulation max = 125°C, stalled at 3 A (current-limited).
1. P_copper = I²Ra = 3² × 1.2 = 10.8 W.
2. ΔT_final (if it ran forever at this current) = P × Rth = 10.8 × 14 = 151.2°C → final temp = 25 + 151.2 = 176.2°C — way above the 125°C limit, so it CANNOT run here forever.
3. Solve for max time: (125−25)/(176.2−25) = 0.661 → 1 − 0.661 = 0.339 → t = −28 × ln(0.339) ≈ 30.2 s before the winding hits its limit.

7. Exam trap library

Motor ID trap

"Poor thermal performance + low torque ripple + simple controller" → always points to DC brushed, not BLDC. The thermal trait is the tell — BLDC has GOOD thermal performance (stator windings).

H-bridge trap

"The series inductor stores energy for regenerative braking." → False. It's a current-smoothing low-pass filter, full stop. Energy storage/recovery happens at the DC bus capacitor level, not this inductor.

Regen braking trap

"Regenerative braking means the motor doesn't heat up." → False. I²R losses still occur — regen is about WHERE the energy goes (back to supply vs wasted in a resistor), not about eliminating losses.

SCR trap

"An SCR/thyristor switch works fine for both AC and DC loads." → False. No natural zero-crossing on DC means it can't turn off — AC only.

Thermal calc trap

Forgetting to ADD friction/iron losses (Tfriction × ω) on top of I²R copper losses when the question gives you a friction torque or no-load loss figure — this changes the final answer noticeably.

Velocity profile trap

"A parabolic velocity profile is optimal for point-to-point motion." → False, generally. Trapezoidal is fastest for given vmax/amax; S-curve (not pure parabolic) is used when jerk must be limited.

8. How these questions could be tweaked on a new exam

Don't just memorize "the answer is DC brushed" — here's how each recurring question type could be reshaped, and how to stay ready for it:

Variant

Motor-ID question, but describing a DIFFERENT motor. Instead of "poor thermal performance," it could say "needs Hall sensors for commutation" (→ BLDC) or "can position itself with no feedback sensor at all" (→ stepper) or "no risk of demagnetization" (→ SyncRM). Use the comparison table in section 2 to reason through ANY trait combination, not just the one you've seen.

Variant

H-bridge question about a DIFFERENT component. Instead of the series inductor, they could ask about the flyback diodes ("what happens if you remove them?" → voltage spike destroys the switch) or about what happens if both switches in one leg turn on simultaneously (a "shoot-through" short circuit across the supply).

Variant

Regen braking question from a different angle. Instead of "does it heat up," they might ask you to identify which QUADRANT a given speed/torque combination falls into, or ask you to calculate the REGENERATED power/energy (P = T × ω, with both negative-relative-to-motoring) during a braking event.

Variant

SCR question about timing instead of AC/DC capability. Could ask about the holding current I_H or latching current I_L directly, or about phase-controlled rectification (firing angle controlling average DC output voltage from an AC supply) instead of just "can it switch DC."

Variant

Thermal+motor calculation with a DIFFERENT starting scenario. Instead of starting from a stalled/current-limited motor, it could give you a RUNNING motor at a specific RPM and load torque (requiring you to first find current via T=Kt·I, possibly factor in back-EMF to find required voltage too), or a cyclic duty-cycle scenario (ON for some time at high load, OFF or low-load for some time, asking for the equivalent continuous power or final steady-state temperature) — same underlying method as section 6, just enter the chain at a different point. Always identify: what current flows? what are ALL the loss sources? what's the time behavior (steady-state vs transient)? — that question triage works regardless of how the scenario is dressed up.

Variant

A totally new open question could combine motor control with GEARBOX reflection (N² rule) — e.g. "a motor drives a load through a gearbox; given load torque and inertia, find required motor torque to accelerate the combined system" — this merges Motor Control with the Drive Systems topic. Don't mentally silo topics; the exam likes to combine them.
