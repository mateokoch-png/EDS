1. The gearbox N² rule

Define N = ω_motor / ω_load (how many times faster the motor spins than the output shaft). A real gearbox is (approximately) lossless, so power in = power out: P = T × ω stays the same on both sides.

Speed: ω_load = ω_motor / N — the gearbox divides speed down by N.
Torque: T_load = T_motor × N — torque is multiplied UP by N (this is the whole point of a gearbox — trade speed for torque).
Reflected torque (seen at the motor): T_reflected = T_load / N — flip the above around to see what torque the motor "feels" from the load.

Why inertia scales by N², not N

This is the part that trips people up. Inertia isn't a force or torque — it's resistance to ANGULAR ACCELERATION (T = J·α). Let's derive it instead of just memorizing it:

Energy stored in a spinning mass: E = ½ J ω². The load's kinetic energy must "appear" the same whether you calculate it using the load's own (J_L, ω_L) or using an equivalent inertia (J_reflected) spinning at the MOTOR's speed ω_motor (since that's the frame we want to reflect into):

½ J_L ω_L²  =  ½ J_reflected ω_motor²

Substitute ω_L = ω_motor / N:

½ J_L (ω_motor/N)² = ½ J_reflected ω_motor²

The ω_motor² cancels from both sides, leaving:

J_reflected = J_L / N²

The intuition in one sentence: speed gets divided by N (linear), but kinetic energy depends on speed SQUARED — so when you "undo" that speed division to compare energies, the inertia has to be divided by N² to make the energy match up.

So a high gear ratio makes even a heavy load feel very light to the motor, inertia-wise — but the trade-off is the motor has to spin N times faster to achieve the same output speed, and any torque the motor produces only translates to 1/N of that torque at the load shaft (wait — no, torque MULTIPLIES by N on the output side, re-read the speed/torque box above; reflected torque going the OTHER way, from load to motor, divides by N).

2. Inertia matching

Given a fixed motor (with its own rotor inertia J_m) and a load (J_L) connected through an adjustable gear ratio N, what N gives the best acceleration performance?

Total inertia the motor has to accelerate = J_m + J_reflected = J_m + J_L/N². As N increases, J_reflected shrinks — seems like "more N is always better" at first glance.
But increasing N also means the motor has to spin faster to get the same output speed, AND any torque limit (max motor torque) gets divided by N before it reaches the load. There's a genuine trade-off, and calculus shows the optimum (fastest acceleration of the load) happens at:

J_m = J_L / N²  (i.e. J_motor = J_reflected)

In words: pick the gear ratio so the motor's OWN inertia equals the load's REFLECTED inertia. At this point, exactly half the motor's torque output goes into accelerating itself, and half goes into accelerating the (reflected) load — this 50/50 split is what gives the best acceleration for a torque-limited motor.

3. Worked example — the full chain

A motor (J_m = 0.002 kg·m²) drives a load (J_L = 0.8 kg·m², T_L = 16 N·m constant) through a gearbox with N = 8. Find the motor torque needed to accelerate the system at α_motor = 100 rad/s².

Step 1 — reflect the load inertia: J_reflected = J_L / N² = 0.8 / 64 = 0.0125 kg·m².

Step 2 — total inertia at the motor: J_total = J_m + J_reflected = 0.002 + 0.0125 = 0.0145 kg·m².

Step 3 — torque to accelerate that inertia: T_accel = J_total × α = 0.0145 × 100 = 1.45 N·m.

Step 4 — reflect the load TORQUE (not inertia — divide by N, not N²): T_load_at_motor = T_L / N = 16 / 8 = 2 N·m.

Step 5 — total motor torque required: T_motor = T_accel + T_load_at_motor = 1.45 + 2.0 = 3.45 N·m.

The single most common mistake: reflecting TORQUE by N² (should be ÷N) or reflecting INERTIA by N (should be ÷N²). They scale differently — keep that straight by re-deriving from energy conservation if you forget, rather than guessing.

4. Electrical vs pneumatic vs hydraulic drive systems

Actually recurring on past exams

This exact True/False pair has appeared multiple times: "Statement 1: pneumatics are interesting from a safety perspective in overload (air is compressible), but that compressibility hurts accurate positioning. Statement 2: electrical drives are often used because efficiency of energy conversion is higher than pneumatic/hydraulic." → Answer: both statements are TRUE.

5. Four-quadrant drives (anti-parallel bridges)

Two thyristor bridges, connected so current can flow through the motor in EITHER direction without needing to mechanically reverse anything. A switch-over control block ensures only ONE bridge is actively conducting at any moment (if both conducted simultaneously, current would circulate destructively between them).

This gives full four-quadrant capability: motoring forward, regen braking from forward, motoring reverse, regen braking from reverse — all from a single drive setup.

6. Exam trap library

N² trap

"Reflected torque also scales by N²." → False. Torque scales by N (or 1/N depending on direction). Only INERTIA scales by N² — because inertia relates to energy (∝ ω²), torque doesn't.

Inertia matching trap

"More gear reduction is always better for acceleration." → False. There's an optimum (J_motor = J_reflected); past that point, the motor spends too much torque accelerating itself and the trade-off reverses.

Pneumatic trap

"Pneumatic systems are bad for positioning AND bad for overload safety." → False — they're bad for positioning (compressibility = imprecise) but actually GOOD for overload safety for the same reason (compressible air cushions sudden overloads).

Anti-parallel bridge trap

"Both bridges can conduct simultaneously for faster switching." → False. Simultaneous conduction creates a circulating current path between the two bridges — a switch-over block must ensure only one conducts at a time.

7. How these questions could be tweaked

Variant

Combined motor+gearbox open question. Given motor specs (Kt, Ra, Rth) AND a gearbox+load (N, J_L, T_L), find required motor current/voltage/temperature rise — chains this entire topic into the Motor Control thermal calculation from the previous topic. Always check: does the question mention a gearbox or ratio at all? If so, reflect torque/inertia FIRST, then run the usual motor current → losses → temperature chain.

Variant

Reverse the unknown. Instead of "find required motor torque," it could give you the motor's MAX torque and ask you to find the MAXIMUM achievable load acceleration, or ask you to solve for the gear ratio N that satisfies an inertia-matching condition given specific J_m and J_L values.

Variant

Drive system comparison from a different angle. Instead of the efficiency/positioning T/F pair, could ask you to rank all three drive types by force density, or describe a specific application (e.g. "explosive atmosphere, simple clamping action") and ask which drive type fits best — same underlying comparison table, different framing.

Variant

Four-quadrant question about energy flow instead of bridge structure. Could ask you to identify which quadrant a given speed/torque scenario is in (same diagram as the Motor Control regen-braking section), or to calculate regenerated power during a braking event (P = T × ω).
